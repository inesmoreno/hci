Observation and Proposal


1. Observation

In order to observe two user groups (presenters and participants), we watched different recorded Zoom presentations and generated sticky notes about our findings on white board software Miro to define mental models. In the same videos, we observed the presenters and participants at the same time. To validate our findings, we often revisited a particular scene and watched in pairs.

  • Setting: Recorded lecture (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpTDF4w-6Yc)
  • Setting: Tutorial session (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jMMK9KKgOWY&t=669s)

After listing all distinguished tasks on the white boards, we grouped them by behavior affinity.



Presenters struggle to solicit and assess participant engagement and responses to presentation. Presenters have been shown to ask (or sometimes appear to plead in the instances of educational presentations) for participant feedback. Participants that are familiar with the presentation do manage to provide feedback through unmuting and speaking, messaging in chat, or using features like hand raising. Unfortunately, most participants don‚Äôt provide any feedback to the presenter and have their camera off, seemingly disengaged. This appears to make presenters feel uncomfortable. On the other hand, when presenters want to assess participant reaction (for instance by reading the chat), it often interrupts their presentation.


Presenters

As far as presenters are concerned, they can be grouped by two core attributes:
prior knowledge of video conferencing and degree in interest to engage people.



Participants

For participants, we can group them into several categories based on two core attributes: participation and focus



In order to keep it manageable within the allotted time frame, we selected core groups to focus for this project. For presenters, we decided to target those with low and medium prior knowledge of video-conferencing tools , as well as those who look forhigh participation from their audience. For participants, we targeted both shy and active participants that are focused on the presentation.


2. Problem identified


The inability for presenters and participants to engage and have dialogue with one another is a major issue with video conferencing. The lack of low risk, non-disruptive, simple-to-use features that allow participants to comfortably engage with presentations results in lower levels of engagement. For many presenters, participant engagement and reaction serves as a measure of the presentation‚Äôs success. Unfortunately, because of the suppressed participant reaction, presenters can be made to feel boring and awkward. Furthermore, this depression of participation can make participating even harder as the norm becomes silence and turned off webcams, making speaking up or asking a question all the more intimidating and daunting to participants. This lack of engagement is especially troublesome for presentations with educational objectives as engagement is a necessary component for learning. So far, we have yet to find a product that finds a way to support participants' engagement and interaction with the presentation.

4. Use cases

Presenters
  • This presenter wants to host an online work training event for employees.
  • Presenter opens the video conferencing app, logs in using their credentials, and starts an online meeting by clicking the ‚ÄúStart Meeting‚Äù button.
  • They are presented with a link to the meeting, share it with their audience, and waits for people to join..
  • As the participants trickle in, the presenter uses this time to get to know the audience by asking some questions, like: ‚ÄúAre you familiar with the presentation topic? Raise your hand if yes‚Äù
  • Now that all the participants have arrived, the presenter starts a quick poll about the topic they are covering where participants vote using A/B/C/D. The presenter is able to quickly assess their response as the results live-tally on screen and reacts accordingly.
  • During the more technical phase of the presentation, the presenter notes that a lot of participants have anonymously reported they are beginning to feel lost. This cues the presenter to slow down and cover the material with more details so participants don‚Äôt feel lost.
  • At the end of the presentation, the participants overwhelmingly give the presenter clapping emojis and the presenters feels satisfied knowing they did a good job!

Participants
  • The participant follows the link given by the presenter to attend a class and joins the class early.
  • The class already has quite a few students in it, so this participant doesn‚Äôt feel comfortable using their microphone to say ‚ÄòHi‚Äô to the others. Instead, they give a ‚Äúwave‚Äù emoji to signal their presence.
  • As the class is starting, the teacher asks the students to tell them how they feel with an anonymous emoji reaction. This student knows they‚Äôre anonymous so they answer honestly with a sleepy face emoji. They watch as the live tally of student's emoji reactions pour in: many students are responding with a ‚Äúmeh‚Äù, ‚Äúsleepy‚Äù or x_x face emoji. This student feels a little less alone.
  • The teacher asks review questions covering content in the previous class. Again, because the poll is anonymous and the button to respond is on the main app interface, they respond.
  • During the discussion of new content, the student begins to feel lost. They emote anonymously that they feel lost and see the tally of lost feeling students increase by one. Soon the number begins to climb to about ¬º of the class, so the teacher pauses their instruction to solicit clarifying questions from students.
  • Students begin raising their hand and a queue is populated based on first come first serve. This student was 3rd to raise their hand so they wait for their turn. It turns out that the first question asked is the same as theirs, so they lower their hand which automatically removes them from the queue of questions.
  • By the end of the class, the professor again asks students to anonymously respond with how well they understood that day's content with an emoji poll. The student responds with a green checkmark.



5. Related products


Zoom

Due to its high level of security and a variety of useful features that support educators, Zoom has been used as a virtual classroom platform in many educational institutions including McGill University. On Zoom, students are able to provide feedback to their lecturers via emoji and express their reactions by sending a clap or thumbs up. Moreover, students can display a ‚Äúraise hand‚Äù icon if they want to get permission to speak during the class. These various features of Zoom allow students to transform their behavior in off-line class into a virtual class so that students are able to feel strong telepresence throughout the class.

However, These emoji buttons are scattered on the software so it sometimes invites undesired confusion. For example, you can express ‚Äúthumbs up‚Äù via emoji of reactions tab or participants list. Users will likely be confused with the differences between the two. In addition, emojis are often not noticed when a shared screen is occupied on an educator‚Äôs screen. Therefore, students often don‚Äôt get a chance to interact with an educator.

The current layout of Zoom is poorly designed. Students need to manually place windows of participants and chat on their screen when needed as Zoom only provides one default layout; chatbox on the left corner, participants list on the left top and screen shared on the middle. Therefore, on a default layout if a chat box is too small due to one‚Äôs laptop size, having a discussion in a chat is somewhat challenging.

6. Product comparison



One of the main advantages of the system we are proposing is the ability for the participants to react and respond anonymously during a presentation, without garnering overwhelming attention and/or disrupting the class.

Furthermore, we propose an easy visualization of participant‚Äôs reactions and responses for the presenters, with no need of switching to different tabs, or changing the view mode.

Finally, our system is intended to be free and open-source so presenters can customize it to their needs and can use it with any pre-existing video-conferencing tool without having to spend extra dollars.

The system will benefit our personas in the following ways:

For Diane, the student member of a peer-teaching community, the system will allow her to easily visualize her peer‚Äôs reactions when presenting a topic during one of their teaching sessions. She will be able to get a sense of how many participants are following and which are the reactions to her presentation. That way, she can immediately pause and give further explanations when the crowd is feeling lost or speed up her presentation if they‚Äôre feeling bored.

On the other hand, Dakota will be able to interact with their audience when presenting during class. They will be able to see when participants found their jokes funny, and if they are liking the content they are presenting. They will also be able to poll their audience to make sure they are following their presentation and realize interactive exercises that engage everyone with the content.

With our system, Jamie (the active convention participant) can actively participate in discussions around business presentations, he will be able to raise his hand and be sure he gets noticed. He won‚Äôt have to interrupt the presentation in order to ask questions and will get a chance to be designated to speak in an orderly manner. Furthermore, he will be able to see other participants reactions and not feel like he is alone in the presentation.

Finally, Priscilla, the shy student who is afraid of sounding stupid when participating in discussions, giving a wrong answer when asked or asking for clarification when she is lost, will benefit from our system because she will be able of anonimously vote in quick polls as well as expressing her feelings and reacting to the presentation without being noticed by other participants and/or the presenter.
